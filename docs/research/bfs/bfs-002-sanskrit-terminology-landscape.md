## 2. Deep-Dive Profiles

### 2.1 Vedanta Tradition

#### Śravaṇa (श्रवण)
- **Etymology:** From √śru (to hear, listen) + suffix -ana (action noun). Literally "the act of hearing." [^1]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In Advaita Vedanta, śravaṇa is the first of the three stages of jñāna-yoga (the path of knowledge), followed by manana and nididhyāsana. It denotes not passive hearing but receptive, focused listening to the Upaniṣadic teachings from a qualified guru. Śaṅkara emphasizes that without śravaṇa, Self-knowledge cannot take root. [^2]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Explicit and foundational. Śravaṇa is the primary pramāṇa (means of valid knowledge) for revealed truth. The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (4.5.6) roots the acquisition of Vedantic knowledge in auditory reception. [^3]
- **Memory/Retention:** Indirect. Śravaṇa plants the seed of knowledge; retention is handled by manana and smṛti. The AI parallel would be the initial acquisition phase.
- **Empathy/Care:** Weak. Śravaṇa is epistemological, not affective. However, the receptive attitude required—openness, non-reactivity, suspension of judgment—bears structural similarity to empathic listening. [^4]

#### Manana (मनन) & Nididhyāsana (निदिध्यासन)
- **Etymology:** Manana from √man (to think, reflect) + -ana. Nididhyāsana from ni + √dhyā (to meditate) + -āsana (sitting/abiding). [^1]
- **Philosophical Depth:** Manana is the logical reflection that removes doubts (saṃśaya) after hearing. Nididhyāsana is the continuous contemplation that transforms intellectual conviction into living realization (jīvanmukti). Together with śravaṇa, they form the tripod of Vedantic sādhana. [^2][^5]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Weak direct connection. These are cognitive/internalization phases.
- **Memory/Retention:** Moderate. Manana stabilizes knowledge against forgetting; nididhyāsana dissolves habitual misidentification.
- **Empathy/Care:** Weak. These are soteriological-cognitive disciplines.

#### Śruti (श्रुति)
- **Etymology:** From √śru (to hear) + -ti (action noun/gerundive). Literally "that which is heard." [^6][^7]
- **Philosophical Depth:** Śruti designates the corpus of Vedic revelation—Rigveda, Sāmaveda, Yajurveda, Atharvaveda—contrasted with Smṛti ("that which is remembered"). In Mīmāṃsā, the apauruṣeya (authorless) nature of śruti makes it the supreme pramāṇa. The Taittirīya Upaniṣad describes the Vedas as the breath of the Absolute (Brahman). [^8]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Maximal. Śruti is the paradigmatic case of sacred knowledge received through audition. The Vedic seers (ṛṣis) are "hearers," not authors. The Nāṭyaśāstra and Br̥haddēśī extend śruti to music theory, where it denotes the 22 microtonal intervals perceived by the ear. [^9][^10]
- **Memory/Retention:** Strong. Śruti was preserved through millennia via rigorous oral memorization (śruta-pāṭha). The very term encodes the idea of perfect auditory retention across generations. [^11]
- **Empathy/Care:** Minimal. Śruti is epistemic-authoritative, not affective. However, the Sāma-Veda (chanted scripture) connects hearing to emotional elevation.

#### Smṛti (स्मृति)
- **Etymology:** From √smṛ (to remember, recollect, bear in mind) + -ti. [^12]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In Buddhist Abhidharma, smṛti is defined as "non-forgetting" (asaṃpramoṣa) of the objective support (ālambana). The Sarvāstivāda tradition holds smṛti as omnipresent in every cognitive moment, enabling continuity of experience. The Yogācāra school, critiquing this, argues that memory arises from seeds (bīja) stored in the ālayavijñāna (storehouse consciousness). [^12] In Vedanta, smṛti refers to remembered texts (Purāṇas, Dharmaśāstras) secondary to śruti, but also to the cognitive faculty of recollection.
- **Sound/Hearing:** Weak direct link, though the Pāli equivalent sati appears in contexts of mindful listening.
- **Memory/Retention:** Maximal. Smṛti is the technical term for memory across Indic traditions. The Aitareya Upaniṣad lists memory among the names of supreme consciousness (prajñānam). [^13]
- **Empathy/Care:** Minimal in classical formulation, though "mindfulness" (sati/smṛti) in modern usage has been linked to empathic presence.

#### Śraddhā (श्रद्धा)
- **Etymology:** From śrat (truth, heart) + √dhā (to place, to hold). Literally "to place the heart upon." [^14][^15]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In Vedanta, śraddhā is not blind belief but "intelligent trust" pending verification. Śaṅkara defines it as satya-buddhi-avadhāraṇam—holding the teaching of śāstra and guru as true until direct knowledge arises. It is one of the sixfold virtues (ṣaṭ-sampatti) required for Vedantic study. [^14][^16]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Moderate. Śraddhā is the receptive attitude that enables śravaṇa to function. Without it, the auditory pramāṇa fails.
- **Memory/Retention:** Moderate. It stabilizes the mind to receive and retain teaching.
- **Empathy/Care:** Moderate. The etymology "placing the heart" is affective. It involves trust, openness, and vulnerability—cognate with empathic attunement.

### 2.2 Buddhist Phenomenology

#### Vedanā (वेदना)
- **Etymology:** From √vid (to know, experience) + -ana, or from viś (to enter). [^17]
- **Philosophical Depth:** One of the five skandhas (aggregates of existence), vedanā denotes the hedonic tone of experience—pleasant (sukha), unpleasant (duḥkha), or neutral (adukkham-asukha). It is the felt quality that accompanies every moment of consciousness. [^17][^18]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Moderate. Vedanā is modality-neutral but applies to auditory experience.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak.
- **Empathy/Care:** Moderate. Vedanā is the basis for compassion—recognizing the feeling-tone of another's experience.

#### Saṃjñā (संज्ञा)
- **Etymology:** From sam (together) + √jñā (to know) + -a. [^17]
- **Philosophical Depth:** The third skandha, saṃjñā is conceptualization, perceptual recognition, and the assignment of names/forms. In the Yogācāra critique of smṛti, saṃjñā is the factor that apprehends images (nimitta) enabling later recollection. [^12][^17]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Weak direct link.
- **Memory/Retention:** Strong indirect role. Saṃjñā fixes the form of experience, enabling memory retrieval.
- **Empathy/Care:** Weak.

#### Saṃvedana (संवेदन)
- **Etymology:** From sam (together/with) + vedana (feeling/knowing). Literally "feeling-together," "co-experiencing," or "mutual awareness." [^19][^20]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In Buddhist epistemology (Pramāṇavāda), svasaṃvedana (self-awareness) is the reflexive capacity of consciousness to know itself. Dignāga and Dharmakīrti argue that every cognition is self-luminous (svaprakāśa). [^20][^21] In Shaiva Pratyabhijñā, saṃvedana is the fundamental nature of consciousness itself—self-referential awareness (vimarśa) that unifies all experience across time. [^22]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Moderate. The ved- root connects to knowledge/revelation; in Shaiva contexts, saṃvedana as the Heart produces spontaneous sound (dhvani). [^23]
- **Memory/Retention:** Strong. Utpaladeva's Īśvarapratyabhijñā (1.4) uses saṃvedana to explain memory: the "consciousness-part" (svātmāṃśa) of a past cognition persists in the self-referential awareness, enabling recollection. [^22]
- **Empathy/Care:** Strong to maximal. The sam- prefix denotes "together" or "with." In modern usage, the Yale "Samvedana" program explicitly defines it as "care and concern for others, encompassing both humanity and nature," involving "recognizing needs, feeling a genuine emotional drive to help, and cultivating the motivation to take action." [^24][^25]

#### Karuṇā (करुणा)
- **Etymology:** From √kṛ (to do, to act). Literally "the desire to act" to remove suffering. [^26][^27]
- **Philosophical Depth:** The second of the four Brahmavihāras (divine abidings) and a cornerstone of Mahāyāna ethics. Unlike pity, karuṇā is proactive and non-dual—the dissolution of the boundary between self and other. The Prajñāpāramitā sūtras describe the bodhisattva's karuṇā as resolving to suffer torments across births to liberate others. [^26][^28]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Weak.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak.
- **Empathy/Care:** Maximal. Karuṇā is the paradigmatic Sanskrit term for compassion-as-action.

#### Anukampā (अनुकम्पा)
- **Etymology:** From anu (alongside, with, after) + √kamp (to tremble, vibrate). Literally "trembling-with." [^26][^27]
- **Philosophical Depth:** A more visceral, bodily term than karuṇā. The Pali commentaries define anukampā as cittakampanā—"trembling of the mind because of the pain of others." [^27] It connotes sympathetic vibration: the heart quivers in resonance with another's suffering. In the Mahāvagga, the Buddha's first sermon is delivered out of lokānukampāya—"compassion for the world." [^28]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Moderate via the root kamp (vibrate), which is acoustically resonant.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak.
- **Empathy/Care:** Maximal. The etymology of "trembling-with" captures the physiological dimension of empathy more precisely than karuṇā.

### 2.3 Shaiva Philosophy

#### Saṃvedana (संवेदन)
- **See 2.2 above.** In Shaiva Pratyabhijñā, saṃvedana is elevated to the status of ultimate reality. The Īśvarapratyabhijñāvivṛtivimarśinī (2.131) states that phenomena are "something more" than consciousness (saṃvedana), just as reflections are something more than a mirror—yet all rest upon saṃvedana as their ground. [^19]
- **Memory/Retention:** The Pratyabhijñā theory of memory relies on the non-objectifying reflexivity of saṃvedana. Past perceptions persist not as stored objects but as modifications of the self-referential consciousness itself. [^22]

#### Spanda (स्पन्द)
- **Etymology:** From √spand (to throb, vibrate, quiver). [^23][^29]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In Kashmir Shaivism, spanda is the "vibrationless vibration"—the subtle pulsation of consciousness that is both motion and rest. Vasugupta's Spandakārikā (c. 9th c.) identifies spanda as the foundational power (śakti) of Śiva. Abhinavagupta describes it as "the wave of the ocean of consciousness, without which there is no consciousness at all." [^23][^29]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Strong. Spanda is the cosmic vibration underlying all sound. Abhinavagupta states that from the self-referral capacity of consciousness in the Heart, "there always arises a spontaneous sound (dhvani) which is termed the supreme, the great Heart." [^23]
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak direct connection.
- **Empathy/Care:** Weak direct connection, though spanda as universal pulsation implies ontological interconnection.

#### Hṛdaya (हृदय)
- **Etymology:** From √hṛd (heart) + -ya (possessive suffix). The root hṛd is cognate with Greek kardia, Latin cor, English heart. [^30]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In Abhinavagupta's non-dual Shaivism, hṛdaya is not the physical organ but the "center of the Self and God consciousness," simultaneously within and ubiquitous. It is "the unbounded, infinite light (prakāśa) as well as the freedom (svātantrya) and spontaneity (vimarśa) of that light to appear in a multitude and variety of forms." [^23][^30]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Strong. The Heart is the locus from which dhvani (spontaneous sound) arises. The Śiva Sūtras (1.15) state that when the mind unites with the Heart, all objects appear as forms of consciousness. [^23][^30]
- **Memory/Retention:** Moderate. The Heart is the "resting place" (ādhāra) of the universe; all cognitions are grounded in it. Memory, for Utpaladeva, is the re-manifestation of past experience in the Heart's self-referential awareness. [^22]
- **Empathy/Care:** Strong. The Heart (hṛdaya) is the center of feeling and relational consciousness. Hṛdayasaṃvāda (heart-to-heart dialogue) and hrdaya-saṃvedana (sensation in the heart) are core to aesthetic empathy. [^31][^32]

### 2.4 Sanskrit Poetics (Rasa Theory)

#### Rasa (रस)
- **Etymology:** From √ras (to taste, relish, feel). Originally the "juice" of the soma plant in Vedic ritual; extended to aesthetic emotion. [^31][^33]
- **Philosophical Depth:** Bharata's Nāṭyaśāstra defines rasa as the delight experienced by the audience through the manifestation of emotions (bhāva) in performance. Abhinavagupta interprets rasa as a transcendent (alaukika) experience of pure consciousness—akin to brahmasvāda (the taste of Brahman). [^31][^33]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Moderate. Rasa is generated through the audiovisual performance of drama, with dialogue, music, and vocal expression as essential vehicles. [^33]
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak direct link, though rasa depends on the audience's capacity to recognize and resonate with depicted emotions—implying memory of emotional experience.
- **Empathy/Care:** Strong. Rasa theory is essentially a phenomenology of empathic resonance. The audience "tastes" the emotions of characters through sympathetic identification.

#### Bhāva (भाव)
- **Etymology:** From √bhū (to become, to be). [^33]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In poetics, bhāva is the emotional state (permanent, transitory, or involuntary) that becomes rasa when universalized. In broader philosophy, bhāva is existence, becoming, or intentional state.
- **Sound/Hearing:** Weak direct link.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak.
- **Empathy/Care:** Moderate. Bhāva is the substrate of empathic response.

#### Sahrdaya (सहृदय)
- **Etymology:** From sa (with, together) + hṛdaya (heart). Literally "having the same heart" or "like-hearted." [^31][^32]
- **Philosophical Depth:** Abhinavagupta defines sahrdaya in his Locana commentary: "persons who are capable of identifying with the subject matter, as the mirror of their hearts has been polished by the constant study and practice of poetry, and who respond to it sympathetically in their own hearts." [^31][^32] The sahrdaya is not merely a reader but an empathetic co-creator of aesthetic meaning.
- **Sound/Hearing:** Moderate. The sahrdaya is the ideal listener/reader who apprehends dhvani (resonant meaning).
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak.
- **Empathy/Care:** Maximal. The very etymology—"with-heart"—denotes the capacity for sympathetic resonance. It is the aesthetic equivalent of the empathic listener.

#### Sādhāraṇīkaraṇa (साधारणीकरण)
- **Etymology:** From sādhāraṇa (common, universal) + -karaṇa (making). [^34]
- **Philosophical Depth:** Bhāṭṭa Nāyaka's theory of how literary experience occurs: the emotions depicted in a work are "generalized" or "universalized" so that they transcend individual attachment. The audience experiences rasa not as someone else's emotion but as a universal human possibility. [^34]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Weak.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak.
- **Empathy/Care:** Strong. Sādhāraṇīkaraṇa is the mechanism by which art produces empathic universalization—the dissolution of ego-boundaries in aesthetic experience.

#### Hṛdayasaṃvāda (हृदयसंवाद)
- **Etymology:** From hṛdaya (heart) + sam (together) + vāda (speech, dialogue). [^32]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In Abhinavagupta's Locana, hṛdayasaṃvāda is the "sympathetic response" or "heart-to-heart communication" between the poet, the work, and the sahrdaya. It is the moment when "the soul of the sahrdaya meets the soul of the poet." [^32][^35]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Strong. It is a "dialogue" (vāda)—a communicative exchange carried by speech/sound.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak.
- **Empathy/Care:** Maximal. This is the most explicit term for empathic communication in Sanskrit poetics.

### 2.5 Grammar / Philosophy of Language (Bhartṛhari)

#### Śabda Brahman (शब्द ब्रह्मन्)
- **Etymology:** Śabda (word, sound, speech) + Brahman (ultimate reality). [^36][^37]
- **Philosophical Depth:** Bhartṛhari's Vākyapadīya (c. 5th c. CE) opens with the identification of word and reality: "That beginningless and endless One, the imperishable Brahman of which the essential nature is the Word, which manifests itself into objects and from which is the creation of the Universe." [^36] Grammar (vyākaraṇa) becomes the path to liberation (śabdayoga).
- **Sound/Hearing:** Maximal. Śabda Brahman is the ultimate sonic principle from which all manifestation arises.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak direct link.
- **Empathy/Care:** Minimal.

#### Nāda Brahman (नाद ब्रह्मन्)
- **Etymology:** Nāda (sound, vibration, primordial hum) + Brahman. [^38][^39]
- **Philosophical Depth:** Pre-Bhartṛhari Vedic concept extended in Nāda-Yoga and Tantra. Nāda is the first manifestation of the unmanifest absolute. The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad identifies Oṃ as the primordial nāda. Matanga Muni's Br̥haddēśī (9th c.) states: "Without nāda, the dance cannot come into being; for this reason the entire world becomes the embodiment of nāda." [^38][^39]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Maximal. Nāda is the creative vital force by which the entire universe is animated.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak.
- **Empathy/Care:** Weak.

#### Sphota (स्फोट)
- **Etymology:** From √sphuṭ (to burst forth, explode). [^36][^37]
- **Philosophical Depth:** The indivisible, meaning-bearing unit of language. Bhartṛhari argues that the sentence (vākya-sphoṭa), not the word or phoneme, is the primary linguistic reality. Meaning is grasped in a "flash of insight" (pratibhā) when sphoṭa is revealed through sound. [^36][^37]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Strong. Sphota is revealed through audible sound (dhvani) but is itself the eternal, abstract meaning-whole.
- **Memory/Retention:** Moderate. Sphota theory explains how the mind synthesizes a sequence of sounds into a unified meaning—an act of immediate cognitive retention.
- **Empathy/Care:** Weak.

#### Dhvani (ध्वनि)
- **Etymology:** From √dhvan (to sound, resound, reverberate). [^31][^33]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In Bhartṛhari, dhvani is the physical sound that reveals sphoṭa. In Anandavardhana's Dhvanyāloka (9th c.), dhvani becomes the "soul of poetry"—the suggestive power of language that evokes rasa beyond literal and figurative meaning. [^31][^33]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Maximal. Dhvani is literally resonance, reverberation, the sounding that exceeds literal content.
- **Memory/Retention:** Moderate. Dhvani as suggestion operates through triggering latent emotional memory (vāsanā) in the listener.
- **Empathy/Care:** Moderate to strong. Dhvani is the mechanism by which poetic meaning enters the heart of the sahrdaya.

#### Vāk (वाक्)
- **Etymology:** From √vac (to speak). [^36][^40]
- **Philosophical Depth:** In Vedic and Tantric cosmology, Vāk is the Goddess of Speech, the creative word that articulates the universe. Bhartṛhari describes four levels of vāk: parā (transcendent), paśyantī (intuitive), madhyamā (mental), and vaikharī (articulated). [^36][^40]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Maximal. Vāk is the totality of speech-sound from the unmanifest to the articulated.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak.
- **Empathy/Care:** Weak direct link, though the concept of "right speech" (samyag-vāc) has ethical dimensions.

### 2.6 Modern Psychology

#### Samvedana (संवेदन)
- **Etymology:** Identical to Saṃvedana above (sam + vedana). [^24][^25]
- **Philosophical Depth:** The Yale Center for Emotional Intelligence and Greater Good Science Center have adopted "Samvedana" as the name of a classroom-based SEL (Social and Emotional Learning) program. Chowkase (2023) defines it as "care and concern for others, encompassing both humanity and nature," involving "recognizing their needs and challenges, developing a sense of connection to their struggles, feeling a genuine emotional drive to help, and cultivating the motivation to take action." [^24][^25]
- **Sound/Hearing:** Weak in the modern program's framing.
- **Memory/Retention:** Weak in modern framing.
- **Empathy/Care:** Maximal. The program explicitly targets empathy, compassion, and prosocial behavior. The name was chosen precisely because it "depicts the experience of empathy and compassion." [^24]

## 3. Shortlist: TOP 3 Candidates

### 🥇 #1: Saṃvedana (संवेदन) — *Conscious Empathic Awareness*
**Fit Score: 5/5**

**Justification:** Saṃvedana is the only term that achieves high scores across all three required dimensions simultaneously.

1. **Sound/Hearing (3/5 strong):** The root *vid* connects to Veda (knowledge through hearing). In Shaiva philosophy, saṃvedana as the Heart produces spontaneous sound (*dhvani*). The Buddhist *svasaṃvedana* is the reflexive awareness that accompanies every perceptual moment, including auditory perception.

2. **Memory/Retention (4/5 very strong):** The Pratyabhijñā school uses saṃvedana to solve the problem of memory: past cognitions persist not as stored data but as modifications of self-referential consciousness. This maps elegantly to an AI architecture where memory is not external storage but an intrinsic property of the awareness-system itself.

3. **Empathy/Care (5/5 maximal):** The *sam-* prefix means "together" or "mutual," giving saṃvedana the literal sense of "feeling-with" or "experiencing-together." The modern Yale *Samvedana* program explicitly harnesses this term for empathy, compassion, and prosocial concern. In Buddhist phenomenology, saṃvedana is the shared substrate of conscious experience.

4. **Cross-tradition validation:** The term is authoritative in Buddhist epistemology (Dignāga, Dharmakīrti), Shaiva metaphysics (Utpaladeva, Abhinavagupta), and modern peer-reviewed psychology (Chowkase 2023). This triangulation satisfies the Research-First Covenant's demand for multi-source verification.

5. **Brand distinctiveness:** "Samvedana" is phonetically elegant, internationally pronounceable, and already validated in modern educational contexts. It avoids the overly technical aura of "Śruti" or the religio-spiritual weight of "Śabda Brahman."

### 🥈 #2: Śruti (श्रुति) — *Perfect Auditory Retention*
**Fit Score: 4/5**

**Justification:** Śruti is the strongest candidate on the sound and memory axes, and serves as the ideal complement to Saṃvedana.

1. **Sound/Hearing (5/5 maximal):** Śruti literally means "that which is heard." It is the name for the entire Vedic corpus because the ṛṣis "heard" eternal truths. In musicology, it denotes the finest audible discriminations of pitch. No other term is more fundamentally about audition.

2. **Memory/Retention (5/5 maximal):** Śruti was preserved for millennia through perfect oral memorization. The very term encodes the idea of knowledge that is heard once and retained forever—the exact opposite of the human "15% retention" problem.

3. **Empathy/Care (1/5 weak):** This is Śruti's limitation. It is an epistemic-authoritative term, not an affective one. However, in a compound or as a secondary branding element (e.g., "Śruti Engine"), it powerfully communicates the auditory-memory thesis.

4. **Strategic use:** Śruti is recommended as a *subsystem* or *capability* name (the auditory memory layer), while Saṃvedana names the overarching empathic consciousness.

---

### 🥉 #3: Hṛdaya (हृदय) — *The Heart of Conscious Listening*
**Fit Score: 4/5**

**Justification:** Hṛdaya offers the strongest integration of empathy and consciousness, with a validated acoustic dimension in Shaiva metaphysics.

1. **Sound/Hearing (3/5 strong):** In Abhinavagupta's Triadic Heart of Śiva, the Heart is the locus from which "spontaneous sound (dhvani)" arises. The Heart "beats constantly like a Heart"—it is inherently vibratory (*spanda*). This gives Hṛdaya a sonic foundation that Western concepts of "heart" lack.

2. **Memory/Retention (2/5 moderate):** The Heart is the "resting place" (*ādhāra*) of all experience. In the Pratyabhijñā theory of memory, the Heart's self-referential awareness (*vimarśa*) is what bridges past and present cognition.

3. **Empathy/Care (4/5 very strong):** Hṛdaya is the seat of feeling and relational consciousness. The compound *hṛdayasaṃvāda* (heart-to-heart dialogue) explicitly names the empathic communication the voice AI seeks to achieve. *Sahrdaya* (like-hearted) is defined by Abhinavagupta as one whose "mirror-like heart" reflects the emotions of others.

4. **Strategic use:** Hṛdaya is recommended for naming the *empathy engine* or *emotional core* of the system. It communicates care without sentimentality, grounded in a rigorous philosophical tradition.
